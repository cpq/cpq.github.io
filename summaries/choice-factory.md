# The Choice Factory

Richard Shotton’s *The Choice Factory* is a playbook for applying behavioral science directly to marketing decisions - pricing, messaging, UX, and positioning. The key idea is simple: people don’t make rational choices, but they do make predictable ones. If you understand those patterns, you can design campaigns and products that align with how decisions actually happen, not how we wish they did.

## 1. Social Proof
What it is: People follow what others are doing.  
Example: Hotel guests were more likely to reuse towels when told that most other guests did so.  
Application: Use “most popular”, customer counts, reviews, or usage stats to validate choice.

## 2. Loss Aversion
What it is: Losses feel stronger than equivalent gains.  
Example: Messaging framed as “avoid losing” outperformed equivalent “gain” framing.  
Application: Frame messaging around avoiding loss rather than achieving gain.

## 3. Anchoring
What it is: The first number sets the reference point.  
Example: People asked to write down the last digits of their phone number gave higher bids for products if those digits were higher.  
Application: Show higher-priced options first to make others feel cheaper.

## 4. Framing Effect
What it is: Presentation changes perception.  
Example: Meat described as “75% lean” sold better than “25% fat”.  
Application: Reframe identical facts in a more favorable way.

## 5. Decoy Effect
What it is: A third option shifts preference.  
Example: The Economist subscription offer increased uptake of the premium option by adding a worse-priced print-only decoy.  
Application: Introduce a strategically worse option to push premium choices.

## 6. Availability Heuristic
What it is: People rely on what comes to mind easily.  
Example: People overestimate causes of death that receive more media coverage.  
Application: Use vivid, memorable examples to shape perception.

## 7. Salience Bias
What it is: Attention goes to what stands out.  
Example: Highlighted or visually distinct information is more likely to be chosen.  
Application: Highlight key selling points visually and prominently.

## 8. Priming
What it is: Prior exposure influences decisions.  
Example: People exposed to words associated with age walked more slowly afterward.  
Application: Set context with imagery or language before presenting the offer.

## 9. Halo Effect
What it is: One positive trait influences overall perception.  
Example: Attractive or well-presented brands are perceived as higher quality.  
Application: Invest in strong branding to lift perceived product quality.

## 10. Authority Bias
What it is: People trust experts.  
Example: Participants were more likely to follow instructions from someone in a lab coat.  
Application: Use endorsements, certifications, or expert opinions.

## 11. Scarcity
What it is: Limited availability increases desirability.  
Example: Cookies from a nearly empty jar were rated as more desirable than identical cookies from a full jar.  
Application: Use urgency cues like limited stock or time-based offers.

## 12. Reciprocity
What it is: People return favors.  
Example: Waiters who gave customers a free mint received higher tips.  
Application: Offer free value upfront to increase likelihood of conversion.

## 13. Commitment & Consistency
What it is: People align with prior actions.  
Example: Homeowners who agreed to a small sign were more likely to later accept a large sign.  
Application: Get small commitments first to increase future compliance.

## 14. Endowment Effect
What it is: Ownership increases perceived value.  
Example: People demanded more money to sell a mug they owned than they would pay to buy it.  
Application: Use free trials or demos to create a sense of ownership.

## 15. Contrast Effect
What it is: Perception depends on comparison.  
Example: Expensive items make moderately priced ones seem cheaper when shown first.  
Application: Position products relative to higher-priced or lower-value alternatives.

## 16. Effort Heuristic
What it is: Effort signals value.  
Example: People rated IKEA furniture more highly because they assembled it themselves.  
Application: Emphasize craftsmanship, process, or time investment.

## 17. Distinctiveness Bias
What it is: Unique items are more memorable.  
Example: Von Restorff effect - items that stand out are more likely to be remembered.  
Application: Break category norms to stand out in crowded markets.

## 18. Peak-End Rule
What it is: Experiences are judged by peak and ending.  
Example: Patients rated painful procedures more positively when the ending was less painful.  
Application: Design standout moments and strong finishes in customer journeys.

## 19. Present Bias
What it is: Immediate rewards outweigh future benefits.  
Example: People choose smaller immediate rewards over larger delayed ones.  
Application: Highlight instant gratification in offers.

## 20. Affect Heuristic
What it is: Emotions drive decisions.  
Example: People judged risks and benefits based on how they felt about something rather than data.  
Application: Lead with emotional appeal, support with rational proof.

## 21. Mere Exposure Effect
What it is: Familiarity increases liking.  
Example: Repeated exposure to nonsense words increased preference for them.  
Application: Ensure consistent, repeated brand exposure.

## 22. Verbatim Effect
What it is: People remember gist, not details.  
Example: People recall the meaning of a message rather than exact wording.  
Application: Focus on one clear, memorable message.

## 23. Frequency Illusion
What it is: Once noticed, something appears more frequent.  
Example: After learning a new word, people suddenly notice it everywhere.  
Application: Maintain consistent creative to reinforce perceived ubiquity.

## 24. Default Bias
What it is: People stick with pre-set options.  
Example: Organ donation rates are higher in countries with opt-out defaults.  
Application: Make your preferred choice the default selection.

## 25. Sunk Cost Fallacy
What it is: Past investment drives continued commitment.  
Example: People continue watching bad movies because they paid for the ticket.  
Application: Encourage early investment of time or effort to reduce churn.
